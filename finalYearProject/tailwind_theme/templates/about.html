1. CP (Chest Pain Type)

Type of chest pain experienced.

  • 0: Typical angina
  • 1: Atypical angina
  • 2: Non-anginal pain
  • 3: Asymptomatic
Chest pain type helps assess heart risk and whether pain is heart-related.

2. Trestbps (Resting Blood Pressure)

Blood pressure measured while resting (mm Hg). Normal is ~120/80 mm Hg.

High resting BP increases strain on the heart and blood vessels.

3. Chol (Serum Cholesterol)

Amount of cholesterol in the blood. Healthy level: < 200 mg/dl.

High cholesterol can lead to artery blockage and heart disease.

4. FBS (Fasting Blood Sugar)

Blood sugar after fasting:

  • 1: â‰¥ 120 mg/dl
  • 0: < 120 mg/dl
Elevated sugar can damage heart-controlling nerves and vessels.

5. Restecg (Resting ECG)

Electrocardiogram results at rest:

  • 0: Normal
  • 1: ST-T abnormality
  • 2: Left ventricular hypertrophy
Helps detect electrical or structural heart issues.

6. Thalach (Maximum Heart Rate Achieved)

Maximum heart rate recorded during stress/exercise (usually ~220 - age).

A low peak rate may indicate limited heart performance.

7. Exang (Exercise-Induced Angina)

Chest pain during physical activity:

  • 1: Yes
  • 0: No
Pain during exercise can signal restricted blood flow to the heart.

8. Oldpeak (ST Depression During Exercise)

Difference in ECG readings at rest vs during exercise (in mV).

Higher values may indicate heart stress or oxygen shortage.

9. Slope (ST Segment Slope)

Shape of the ST ECG line during peak exercise:

  • 0: Upsloping
  • 1: Flat
  • 2: Downsloping
Flat or downsloping lines may suggest impaired heart function.

10. CA (Number of Major Vessels Colored)

Count of major arteries visible using a dye test (0â€“3).

More visible blockages = higher heart disease risk.

11. Thal (Thallium Stress Test Result)

Results from a blood flow imaging test:

  • 1: Normal
  • 2: Fixed defect (permanent damage)
  • 3: Reversible defect (temporary blockage)
Helps determine if there's proper blood flow to the heart muscle.
